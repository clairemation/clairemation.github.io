Making a Hash of Things

In the programming language Ruby, objects rule. Every single piece of data is encoded as an object, and what's more, it's encoded as being contained inside another object. Just like your purse contains your wallet contains your bills, in Ruby it's objects all the way down. And just like a Staples superstore, Ruby offers all kinds of objects to suit different organizational needs.

Two object types are especially useful for holding collections of things: Arrays and Hashes.

Arrays hold a bunch of items in numerical order. Imagine a line of people at a deli, each holding a number. You can talk to any of them just by calling their number. Arrays are super useful for holding a bunch of items that contain the same kind of information, such as employee names:

  1. Smith
  2. Jones
  3. Diaz

Pretty clear, right? Anything that makes sense to put in an ordered list is a good candidate for an Array.

Some collections of items wouldn't be very useful in a numbered list. For instance, try to make sense of this:

  1. Smith
  2. Engineering
  3. 367
  4. B-
  5. Green

This collection would work better if each item got a label instead of a number. And that's what Hashes do. A Hash is a collection of items, like an Array. Except the items are in no particular order, and instead of numbers they have labels, called keys. To summon an item from a Hash, you plug in its key.

Now look at our info:

  • Name: Smith
  • Section: Engineering
  • Office: 367
  • Blood Type: B-
  • Supervisor: Green
